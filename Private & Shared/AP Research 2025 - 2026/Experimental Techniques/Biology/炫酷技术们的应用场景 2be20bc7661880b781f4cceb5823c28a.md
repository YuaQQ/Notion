# 炫酷技术们的应用场景

## 🔬 Classical Molecular Biology Techniques

---

### PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

- **Purpose:** Amplify (copy) a specific DNA segment millions of times from a tiny sample
- **How it works:**
    - **Denaturation** (~95°C): Double-stranded DNA is heated to separate into two single strands
    - **Annealing** (~50–65°C): Short DNA sequences called **primers** bind to complementary regions flanking the target
    - **Extension** (~72°C): DNA polymerase (commonly *Taq* polymerase) synthesizes new DNA from the primers
    - Each cycle doubles the number of copies → **exponential amplification**
- **Key components:** Template DNA, primers, dNTPs, DNA polymerase, thermocycler
- **Applications:** Disease diagnosis, forensics, paternity testing, cloning, COVID-19 testing
- **Analogy:** Like a photocopier for a specific page (gene) in a very large book (genome)

### RT-qPCR (Reverse Transcription Quantitative PCR)

- **Purpose:** Measure the **amount of mRNA** (gene expression levels) in a sample, in real-time
- **How it works:**
    1. **Reverse transcription:** An enzyme called reverse transcriptase converts mRNA → complementary DNA (cDNA)
    2. **qPCR:** The cDNA is amplified by PCR; a fluorescent dye or probe emits light as DNA is copied
    3. **Quantification:** Fluorescence is measured each cycle — more starting mRNA = fluorescence appears earlier (lower Ct value)
- **Key components:** mRNA sample, reverse transcriptase, fluorescent dye (e.g., SYBR Green) or TaqMan probes
- **Applications:** Measuring gene expression, viral load quantification (e.g., HIV, SARS-CoV-2), validating other experiments
- **Difference from regular PCR:** Starts from RNA, not DNA; quantitative (tells *how much*), not just qualitative (yes/no)

### Sanger Sequencing (Chain Termination Sequencing)

- **Purpose:** Determine the exact **nucleotide sequence** of a DNA fragment
- **How it works:**
    1. DNA is amplified in a reaction containing normal dNTPs + a small fraction of **ddNTPs** (dideoxynucleotides, fluorescently labeled)
    2. When a ddNTP is incorporated, chain elongation **stops** (no 3'-OH group)
    3. This produces fragments of all possible lengths, each ending in a labeled base (A, T, G, or C)
    4. Fragments are separated by **capillary electrophoresis** (smallest → fastest)
    5. A laser reads the fluorescent color at the end of each fragment → sequence is determined
- **Key components:** Template DNA, primers, DNA polymerase, four ddNTPs (each labeled differently), capillary electrophoresis instrument
- **Output:** A chromatogram (colored peaks) read as a DNA sequence
- **Applications:** Gene sequencing, mutation detection, species identification, forensic analysis
- **Limitation:** Reads ~500–1,000 bp at a time; not ideal for whole-genome sequencing

### Agarose Gel Electrophoresis

- **Purpose:** Separate DNA (or RNA) fragments by **size**
- **How it works:**
    - DNA is loaded into wells in an agarose gel submerged in a buffer
    - Electric current is applied — DNA (negatively charged) migrates toward the **positive pole**
    - Smaller fragments move faster through the gel matrix; larger fragments move slower
    - DNA is visualized using a fluorescent dye (e.g., ethidium bromide, SYBR Safe)
    - A **DNA ladder** (standard size markers) is run alongside to estimate fragment sizes
- **Applications:** Confirming PCR products, analyzing restriction digests, checking RNA quality
- **Result:** Bands on a gel image; band position = fragment size, band intensity ≈ amount of DNA
- **Illustration**

### SDS-PAGE (Sodium Dodecyl Sulfate–Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis)

- **Purpose:** Separate **proteins** by molecular weight (size)
- **How it works:**
    - SDS (a detergent) coats all proteins with a **uniform negative charge** and denatures them (unfolds them)
    - Proteins are loaded into a **polyacrylamide gel** and current is applied
    - All proteins migrate toward the positive pole; separation is based purely on **size** (smaller = faster)
    - Gels are stained (e.g., Coomassie blue) to visualize protein bands
    - A **protein ladder** is used to estimate molecular weights
- **Applications:** Checking protein purity, estimating protein size, preparing samples for Western blot
- **Key difference from agarose gel electrophoresis:** Separates proteins (not DNA); uses polyacrylamide (finer mesh); SDS ensures charge uniformity
- **Illustration**

### Western Blot

- **Purpose:** Detect and identify a **specific protein** in a complex mixture
- **How it works:**
    1. Proteins are separated by **SDS-PAGE**
    2. Proteins are **transferred** (blotted) from the gel onto a membrane (nitrocellulose or PVDF)
    3. The membrane is **blocked** (e.g., with milk protein) to prevent non-specific antibody binding
    4. A **primary antibody** specific to the target protein is applied
    5. A **secondary antibody** (carrying a detection tag — enzyme or fluorescent label) that binds to the primary antibody is applied
    6. Signal is detected (e.g., chemiluminescence or fluorescence); a **band** appears at the molecular weight of the target protein
- **Applications:** Confirming protein expression, diagnosing diseases (e.g., HIV confirmatory test), studying protein modifications
- **Analogy:** SDS-PAGE sorts proteins by size; Western blot then identifies which band is your protein of interest using antibody "name tags"

### Southern Blot

- **Purpose:** Detect a **specific DNA sequence** in a complex DNA sample
- **How it works:**
    1. DNA is digested with restriction enzymes and separated by **agarose gel electrophoresis**
    2. DNA is **denatured** (made single-stranded) and transferred to a membrane
    3. A labeled **DNA probe** (complementary to the target sequence) is added — it hybridizes to the target
    4. Signal (radioactive or fluorescent) is detected on the membrane
- **Applications:** Gene mapping, detecting gene mutations/deletions, forensic DNA fingerprinting (historically)
- **Named after:** Edwin Southern, who invented it in 1975
- **Memory trick:** Southern = DNA; Northern = RNA; Western = Protein (the "blot" family)

### ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay)

- **Purpose:** Detect and **quantify** a specific protein (antigen or antibody) in a sample
- **How it works (sandwich ELISA):**
    1. A **capture antibody** is coated onto a plate well and binds the target antigen from the sample
    2. A **detection antibody** (also specific to the antigen, linked to an enzyme) is added
    3. An enzyme **substrate** is added → the enzyme converts it to a **colored product**
    4. Color intensity is measured with a spectrophotometer → proportional to antigen concentration
- **Types:** Direct, indirect, sandwich, competitive
- **Applications:** COVID-19 antibody tests, pregnancy tests (hCG detection), HIV testing, allergy testing, measuring cytokines
- **Advantage:** Very sensitive, can process many samples at once (96-well plate format), quantitative

## 🧪 Separation & Purification

---

### Ion Exchange Chromatography

- **Purpose:** Separate and **purify proteins** (or other charged molecules) based on their **net charge**
- **How it works:**
    - A column is packed with **charged resin beads**
        - **Cation exchange:** Negatively charged resin → binds positively charged proteins
        - **Anion exchange:** Positively charged resin → binds negatively charged proteins
    - The sample is loaded; proteins with matching charge **stick** to the resin; others flow through
    - Proteins are eluted (released) by gradually **increasing salt concentration** — salt ions compete for binding sites
    - Proteins with weaker interactions elute first; more tightly bound ones elute later
- **Applications:** Pharmaceutical protein purification, antibody production, removing contaminants
- **Used in conjunction with:** Size exclusion chromatography, affinity chromatography (multi-step purification)
- **Illustration**

## 🔍 Detection & Visualization

---

### FISH (Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization)

- **Purpose:** Detect and **localize** specific DNA or RNA sequences **within intact cells or tissue sections** using fluorescent probes
- **How it works:**
    1. Cells or tissue sections are fixed and **permeabilized** to allow probe entry
    2. DNA/RNA is **denatured** (made single-stranded)
    3. A **fluorescently labeled probe** (complementary to the target sequence) is applied and hybridizes (*in situ* = "in place") to the target
    4. Unbound probes are washed away
    5. The sample is visualized under a **fluorescence microscope** — glowing signals reveal the location of the target sequence
- **Key components:** Fluorescent DNA/RNA probes, fluorescence microscope, fixed cell or tissue samples
- **Applications:**
    - Detecting **chromosomal abnormalities** (e.g., trisomy 21/Down syndrome, cancer translocations like BCR-ABL in CML)
    - **Gene mapping** — determining where a gene sits on a chromosome
    - Counting **gene copy number** (amplification or deletion in tumors)
    - Visualizing **RNA expression patterns** in tissues (RNA-FISH / smFISH)
- **Advantage over blotting:** Preserves **spatial context** — you see exactly *where* in the cell or tissue the target sequence is located
- **Limitation:** Requires knowing the sequence in advance to design probes; limited to a few targets at once in standard FISH

### DNA Microarray

- **Purpose:** Measure the expression level of **thousands of genes simultaneously**
- **How it works:**
    1. mRNA is extracted from two conditions (e.g., cancer vs. normal cells) and converted to **cDNA**
    2. Each sample's cDNA is labeled with a different fluorescent dye (e.g., red and green)
    3. Both are hybridized to a chip (microarray) spotted with thousands of known DNA probes
    4. Each spot represents one gene; fluorescence intensity indicates expression level
    5. **Red** = gene expressed more in sample 1; **Green** = more in sample 2; **Yellow** = equal
- **Applications:** Cancer profiling, drug response studies, identifying disease biomarkers
- **Limitation:** Can only detect known sequences (probe-based); now largely replaced by RNA-seq for discovery

### Flow Cytometry

- **Purpose:** Rapidly analyze **physical and molecular properties of individual cells** in a large population — simultaneously and at high throughput
- **How it works:**
    1. Cells are labeled with **fluorescently tagged antibodies** that bind to specific surface or intracellular proteins (markers)
    2. Cells are passed in single file through a **laser beam** in a fluid stream
    3. Each cell scatters light and emits fluorescence — detectors capture:
        - **Forward scatter (FSC):** Correlates with cell **size**
        - **Side scatter (SSC):** Correlates with cell **granularity/complexity**
        - **Fluorescence channels:** Detect specific protein markers
    4. Data is plotted as scatter plots or histograms; populations of cells are identified and **gated** (sorted into groups)
    5. Some instruments (**cell sorters / FACS**) can physically separate cells into different tubes based on their properties
- **Key components:** Fluorescent antibodies, flow cytometer/laser system, software for data analysis
- **Applications:** Immunophenotyping (identifying immune cell types, e.g., T cells, B cells), cell cycle analysis, apoptosis detection, counting CD4+ T cells in HIV patients, cancer diagnosis
- **Advantage:** Can analyze **millions of cells** per minute; multi-parametric (10–50+ markers simultaneously with modern instruments)
- **FACS (Fluorescence-Activated Cell Sorting):** A type of flow cytometry that physically sorts and collects specific cell populations for downstream experiments

### Immunofluorescence (IF)

- **Purpose:** Visualize the **location of specific proteins** within cells or tissues using fluorescently labeled antibodies and a fluorescence microscope
- **How it works:**
    1. Cells or tissue sections are **fixed** (e.g., with paraformaldehyde) to preserve structure and **permeabilized** to allow antibody entry
    2. **Blocking** is performed to prevent non-specific antibody binding
    3. A **primary antibody** specific to the target protein is applied and binds to it
    4. A **secondary antibody** carrying a **fluorescent dye** (e.g., Alexa Fluor, FITC) that recognizes the primary antibody is applied
    5. The sample is imaged under a **fluorescence microscope** (or confocal microscope for 3D detail)
    - **Direct IF:** Uses a single fluorescently labeled primary antibody (faster, less amplification)
    - **Indirect IF:** Uses unlabeled primary + fluorescent secondary (more common; greater signal amplification)
- **Key components:** Primary antibody, fluorescent secondary antibody, fluorescence/confocal microscope, fixed cells or tissue
- **Applications:** Determining subcellular protein localization (nucleus? cytoplasm? membrane?), studying protein co-localization, detecting viral/bacterial infections in tissue, cancer pathology
- **Advantage:** Preserves **spatial context** within cells; can label multiple proteins simultaneously with different-colored fluorophores
- **Difference from flow cytometry:** IF shows *where* in the cell the protein is (spatial); flow cytometry tells *how many* cells express a protein (quantitative population-level)

## 🧫 Gene Silencing & Regulation

---

### RNAi (RNA Interference)

- **Purpose:** **Silence** (knock down) the expression of a specific gene using small RNA molecules
- **How it works:**
    1. Double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) introduced into the cell is processed by an enzyme called **Dicer** into short pieces called **siRNA** (~21–23 bp)
    2. siRNA is loaded into the **RISC** (RNA-Induced Silencing Complex)
    3. RISC uses one strand of siRNA as a guide to find and **cleave** complementary mRNA
    4. The mRNA is degraded → protein cannot be made → gene is silenced
- **Naturally occurring:** Cells use microRNAs (miRNAs) via a similar pathway to regulate their own gene expression
- **Applications:** Gene function studies ("what happens when we turn off gene X?"), therapeutic development (e.g., siRNA drugs for cholesterol, rare diseases)
- **Limitation:** Transient (temporary) effect; potential off-target silencing

## ✂️ Gene Editing (the CRISPR series)

---

### CRISPR-Cas9

- **Purpose:** **Edit** (cut, delete, correct, or insert) specific DNA sequences in the genome with high precision
- **How it works:**
    1. A **guide RNA (gRNA)** is designed to match the target DNA sequence
    2. The gRNA directs the **Cas9 protein** (a molecular "scissors") to the exact location in the genome
    3. Cas9 makes a **double-strand break** (cuts both strands of DNA)
    4. The cell repairs the break via:
        - **NHEJ** (Non-Homologous End Joining): Error-prone → causes insertions/deletions (indels) → gene **knockout**
        - **HDR** (Homology-Directed Repair): Uses a provided template → precise gene **correction or insertion**
- **Origin:** Adapted from a bacterial immune defense system
- **Applications:** Gene therapy, crop engineering, disease modeling, basic research
- **Advantages over older methods:** Faster, cheaper, easier to design, highly specific
- **Illustrations**

### CRISPRa (CRISPR Activation)

- **Purpose:** **Increase** (activate) the expression of a target gene **without cutting DNA**
- **How it works:**
    - Uses **dCas9** (dead Cas9) — the DNA-cutting ability is disabled but DNA-binding is preserved
    - dCas9 is fused to **transcriptional activators** (e.g., VP64, VPR, SAM complex)
    - The guide RNA directs dCas9-activator to the **promoter** of the target gene
    - Activators recruit transcription machinery → gene is **switched on** or expression is boosted
- **Applications:** Turning on silenced genes, studying gene function, therapeutic upregulation
- **Key feature:** Reversible; does not alter the DNA sequence itself

### CRISPRi (CRISPR Interference)

- **Purpose:** **Decrease** (repress) the expression of a target gene **without cutting DNA**
- **How it works:**
    - Uses **dCas9** fused to **transcriptional repressors** (e.g., KRAB domain)
    - Directed to the promoter or coding region of the target gene
    - Physically blocks RNA polymerase from transcribing the gene → **gene silenced**
- **Comparison to RNAi:** CRISPRi acts at the DNA level (blocks transcription); RNAi acts at the mRNA level (destroys transcript)
- **Applications:** Gene knockdown studies, large-scale functional screens, therapeutic gene silencing
- **Key feature:** Reversible; more specific than RNAi (fewer off-target effects)

### ChIP-seq (Chromatin Immunoprecipitation Sequencing)

- **Purpose:** Identify where specific **proteins** (e.g., transcription factors, histones) **bind on the genome**
- **How it works:**
    1. **Crosslink** proteins to DNA in living cells (using formaldehyde)
    2. Break DNA into small fragments by sonication
    3. Use an **antibody** specific to the protein of interest to **pull down** (immunoprecipitate) protein-DNA complexes
    4. Reverse crosslinks and extract the DNA
    5. **Sequence** the pulled-down DNA using next-generation sequencing
    6. Map reads to the genome → **peaks** indicate where the protein was bound
- **Applications:** Mapping transcription factor binding sites, studying histone modifications, understanding gene regulation and chromatin structure
- **Output:** Genome-wide map of protein-DNA binding locations
- **Illustration**

### ChIP-chip (Chromatin Immunoprecipitation + Microarray)

- **Purpose:** Identify where specific **proteins bind on the genome** — the predecessor to ChIP-seq, using a **DNA microarray** instead of sequencing for detection
- **How it works:**
    1. Steps 1–4 are identical to ChIP-seq: crosslink, fragment, immunoprecipitate with antibody, reverse crosslinks, extract DNA
    2. The pulled-down DNA is **amplified** and labeled with a fluorescent dye
    3. Labeled DNA is hybridized to a **DNA microarray (chip)** pre-spotted with probes tiling the genome
    4. Fluorescence intensity at each probe spot indicates whether that genomic region was bound by the protein
- **Key components:** Same ChIP workflow, DNA microarray, fluorescent labeling, microarray scanner
- **Applications:** Mapping transcription factor binding sites, histone modification profiling (historically)
- **Comparison to ChIP-seq:**
    - ChIP-chip is **limited** to sequences represented on the array (probe-based, no novel discovery)
    - ChIP-seq offers **higher resolution**, greater dynamic range, and no array design required
    - ChIP-chip is now largely **replaced by ChIP-seq** but was instrumental in early epigenomics research

### ChIP-qPCR (Chromatin Immunoprecipitation + qPCR)

- **Purpose:** Validate and **quantify protein binding** at a **specific, known genomic locus** — the targeted, low-throughput alternative to ChIP-seq
- **How it works:**
    1. Steps 1–4 are identical to ChIP-seq: crosslink, fragment, immunoprecipitate with antibody, reverse crosslinks, extract DNA
    2. Instead of sequencing, **qPCR** is performed using primers specific to the genomic region of interest
    3. Signal is compared to an **input control** (total DNA before immunoprecipitation) to calculate **fold enrichment**
    4. High enrichment = the protein of interest was bound at that locus
- **Key components:** Same ChIP workflow, locus-specific primers, qPCR instrument
- **Applications:** Confirming ChIP-seq peaks at specific loci, checking if a transcription factor binds a specific promoter, validating histone modifications at a gene of interest
- **Advantage:** Simple, fast, inexpensive, highly quantitative for known target regions
- **Limitation:** Not genome-wide; requires prior knowledge of where to look; low throughput
- **Relationship to ChIP-seq:** ChIP-qPCR is often used to **validate** ChIP-seq findings at selected loci

### ATAC-seq (Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin with Sequencing)

- **Purpose:** Map **open chromatin regions** (accessible DNA) across the genome — reveals active regulatory elements
- **How it works:**
    1. A hyperactive **transposase enzyme (Tn5)** is added to nuclei
    2. Tn5 preferentially inserts DNA adapters into **open/accessible chromatin** (nucleosome-free regions)
    3. The tagged DNA fragments are extracted and **sequenced**
    4. Reads pile up at open regions → indicates **active promoters, enhancers, and regulatory elements**
- **Applications:** Identifying active regulatory regions, comparing chromatin accessibility between cell types or conditions, predicting gene regulation
- **Advantage:** Requires very few cells (can be done on single cells); much simpler than older methods (DNase-seq)

### Hi-C (Genome-Wide Chromatin Conformation Capture)

- **Purpose:** Map **3D genome organization** — which genomic regions are physically close to each other in the nucleus, regardless of their linear distance on the chromosome
- **How it works:**
    1. **Crosslink** DNA and proteins in the nucleus with formaldehyde to freeze 3D contacts
    2. Digest DNA with a **restriction enzyme**, leaving sticky ends
    3. Fill in and **biotinylate** (label with biotin) the sticky ends, then **ligate** (join) nearby DNA fragments — only fragments that were physically touching get joined
    4. Shear DNA into small pieces; use **streptavidin beads** to pull down biotin-labeled ligation junctions
    5. **Sequence** the paired-end DNA fragments
    6. Map read pairs to the genome → a **contact matrix (heat map)** shows interaction frequency between all pairs of genomic loci
- **Key components:** Formaldehyde crosslinking, restriction enzymes, biotin fill-in, ligation, streptavidin pulldown, paired-end next-generation sequencing
- **Output:** A genome-wide **contact map** visualized as a 2D heat matrix; high-frequency contacts appear as bright spots
- **Key concepts revealed:**
    - **TADs (Topologically Associating Domains):** Genomic regions that preferentially interact within themselves — functional units of chromosome organization
    - **Compartments (A/B):** A compartments = active/open chromatin; B compartments = inactive/closed chromatin
    - **Enhancer–promoter loops:** Direct physical contacts between distal regulatory elements and their target genes
- **Applications:** Understanding gene regulation by 3D genome structure, studying how mutations disrupt chromatin topology in disease, identifying long-range enhancer–gene interactions
- **Relationship to other techniques:** Often integrated with ChIP-seq (histone marks) and ATAC-seq (open chromatin) to build a full picture of genome regulation in 3D

### scRNA-seq (Single-Cell RNA Sequencing)

- **Purpose:** Measure gene expression in **individual cells** — reveals cell-to-cell variability hidden in bulk methods
- **How it works:**
    1. Cells are **isolated** individually (e.g., using droplet microfluidics like 10x Genomics)
    2. Each cell is tagged with a unique **barcode** and mRNA is captured
    3. mRNA is reverse transcribed to cDNA and **sequenced**
    4. Gene expression profiles are generated for **each individual cell**
    5. Computational analysis clusters cells by similarity → identifies **cell types, states, trajectories**
- **Applications:** Discovering new cell types, studying development and differentiation, profiling tumors, understanding immune responses
- **Key advantage over bulk RNA-seq:** Reveals **heterogeneity** — what fraction of cells express a gene and at what level
- **Challenge:** Data is complex; requires significant computational analysis; dropout events (genes not detected due to low mRNA)
- **Data processing pipeline**
    1. **Raw Counts**
    2. **Quality Control (QC)**
        - The first step before any analysis. You filter out low-quality cells based on metrics like number of genes detected per cell, total UMI (transcript) counts, and the fraction of mitochondrial reads (high mito% = dying cell). The goal is to remove empty droplets, doublets (two cells captured together), and damaged cells.
    3. **Normalization & Scaling**
        - Raw counts are normalized (e.g., divided by total counts per cell, then log-transformed) to make cells comparable despite differences in sequencing depth. Scaling centers gene expression to zero mean and unit variance so highly expressed genes don't dominate downstream analyses.
    4. **Highly Variable Gene (HVG) Selection**
        - Instead of using all ~20,000 genes, you select the subset (typically 2,000–5,000) that vary the most across cells. These are the most informative genes for distinguishing cell types.
    5. **Principle Component Analysis (PCA)**
        - Reduces the high dimensionality of the data (thousands of genes → ~50 principal components) by capturing the major axes of variation. PCs are used as input for downstream clustering and visualization — not usually visualized directly for biological interpretation, but essential as a computational intermediate step.
    6. **UMAP or t-SNE (Dimensionality Reduction for Visualization) / Clustering**
        - **UMAP or t-SNE r**educes data further to 2D for visualization. Each dot = one cell; cells with similar expression profiles cluster together. **UMAP** is now strongly preferred over t-SNE because it better preserves global structure. This is the iconic "galaxy plot" you see in scRNA-seq papers.
        - **Clustering** (e.g. **Leiden**, **Louvain**) use graph-based algorithms group cells into clusters based on expression similarity (using PCA-reduced data). Each cluster typically represents a distinct **cell type or state**. Resolution is a tunable parameter — higher resolution = more, finer clusters.
    7. **Marker Gene Identification & Cell Type Annotation**
        - After clustering, you find genes that are **differentially expressed** in each cluster compared to all others (e.g., using Wilcoxon rank-sum test). These "marker genes" are then matched to known biology to annotate clusters with cell type labels (e.g., "Cluster 3 expresses CD3E and CD8A → cytotoxic T cells").
    8. Downstream Analyses
        - **Violin Plots**
            - Show the **distribution of expression** of a specific gene across cell clusters or types. The width of the violin at each level = how many cells have that expression value. Great for showing whether a marker gene is specifically expressed in one cluster vs. broadly across many.
        - **Heatmaps**
            - Display expression of multiple genes across multiple cell clusters simultaneously — rows = genes, columns = cells or clusters, color = expression level. Commonly used to show the top marker genes for each cluster side by side.
        - **Differential Expression Analysis (DEA)**
            - Compares gene expression between two conditions (e.g., treated vs. untreated, disease vs. healthy) within the same cell type. Identifies which genes are upregulated or downregulated. Results are often displayed as **volcano plots** (log fold-change vs. statistical significance).
        - **Trajectory / Pseudotime Analysis (e.g., Monocle, Diffusion Pseudotime)**
            - Orders cells along a continuous developmental path based on expression similarity, inferring a "pseudotime" axis. Used to study **differentiation** — e.g., tracking how a stem cell progressively becomes a neuron. Doesn't require knowing the order in advance.
        - **Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) / Pathway Analysis**
            - After finding differentially expressed genes, you test whether they are enriched in known biological **pathways or gene sets** (e.g., "inflammatory response," "cell cycle"). Converts a gene list into biological meaning.
        - **Cell-Cell Communication Analysis (e.g., CellChat, NicheNet)**
            - Infers which cell types are likely **signaling to each other** based on known ligand-receptor pairs. For example, if macrophages express a ligand and T cells express its receptor, a signaling interaction is predicted.
        - **RNA Velocity (e.g., scVelo)**
            - Uses the ratio of **unspliced (pre-mRNA) to spliced (mature mRNA)** to infer the *direction* of gene expression change — essentially predicting where a cell is heading in its differentiation trajectory. Adds arrows to UMAP plots showing future cell states.
