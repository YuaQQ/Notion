# The Science Thinking Framework

[Original Article](https://www.educationforthinking.org/sites/default/files/pdf/05-02WhatIsScientificThinking.pdf)

1. **Scientific Thinking is Defined as "Knowledge Seeking":** The core of scientific thinking is not a set of facts or what scientists do, but the **process of intentionally seeking to improve one's understanding** of the world. It is an active process ("something people do") aimed at achieving scientific understanding ("something people have").
2. **It is Fundamentally About the Coordination of Theory and Evidence:** The essential, defining activity of scientific thinking is the **explicit and intentional coordination of theories with evidence**. This is a conscious process where evidence is mentally represented as distinct from theory, and its implications for that theory are contemplated. This differs from early childhood "implicit theory revision," which happens unconsciously.
3. **It Develops from Foundational Cognitive Milestones:** The capacity for scientific thinking has its origins in early childhood developments, particularly **Theory of Mind**. The critical milestone is the understanding that an assertion (a claim or theory) is a mental representation that can be **false** and can be evaluated against evidence from external reality. This differentiation of "claim" from "evidence" is the foundational step.
4. **It is a Multi-Phase, Cyclical Process:** Skilled scientific thinking involves a cycle of four interconnected phases:
    - **Inquiry:** Formulating questions and goals for investigation.
    - **Analysis:** Operating on data (e.g., through comparison) to identify patterns.
    - **Inference:** Drawing conclusions based on the analysis of evidence.
    - **Argument:** Debating and justifying claims to others, which leads to revised and strengthened understandings.
5. **Mental Models of Causality are Central:** A key developmental challenge is progressing from an immature **"co-occurrence" model of causality** (where a single factor explaining an event is sufficient) to a mature **additive model**. The mature model understands that multiple variables can act consistently and additively on an outcome, which is necessary for effective investigation and prediction. This underpins the need for strategies like controlling variables.
6. **Meta-Level Understanding is Crucial:** Fully developed scientific thinking depends on **meta-level processes**—thinking about one's own thinking. This includes:
    - **Procedural Meta-Knowledge:** Understanding *why* certain investigative strategies (like controlled comparison) are effective and others are not.
    - **Epistemological Understanding:** Evolving beliefs about the nature of knowledge itself, progressing from *absolutist* (knowledge is certain facts) to *multiplist* (all opinions are equal) to *evaluativist* (knowledge is uncertain but some claims are better supported than others). The evaluativist stance is essential for genuine scientific thinking.
    - **Intellectual Values:** Believing that the process of knowledge seeking is worthwhile and effortful.
7. **It is Inherently Social and Argumentative:** Scientific thinking is most authentic and effective when situated in a social context of **discourse and argument**. Justifying claims to others externalizes meta-level processes, strengthens reasoning, and is the primary mechanism through which knowledge is debated and refined. The question "How do you know?" is central to this process.
8. **Development is Variable and Requires Practice:** Scientific thinking is **not a discrete skill** that appears at a certain age. It is a complex of skills that must be achieved at successively higher levels of complexity over a long period. There is vast intra- and inter-individual variability. Development occurs through **sustained practice and exercise** of these thinking skills, not just through direct instruction of strategies. Microgenetic studies show that change involves a shift in the frequency of using more advanced strategies from a available repertoire.
9. **Education Must Focus on Process and "Why":** Effective science education must move beyond presenting information and focus on engaging students in the process of knowledge seeking. It is "the *why*"—conveying the value of inquiry and the rationale behind strategies—that is most important for development, not just the "what" or "how." This requires teachers to understand the developmental trajectory of these thinking skills.

In summary, the science discipline-specific way of knowing is an intentional, developmental, and social process of coordinating theories with evidence, governed by meta-level understanding, and aimed at the goal of improving knowledge. It is a general-purpose way of thinking that is essential to, but not exclusive to, professional science.

### **Problem Statement Template that can be used to describe and narrow the researcher’s topic of inquiry (Social Science)**

---

- There is a problem in or with the understanding of ___________.
- Despite the belief that _______________________ (something that is general accepted in current scholarship) ___________________ (something that refutes accepted thinking) (provide supporting evidence after this statement)
- This problem has negatively affected our ability to understand _____________because ________________ (provide supporting evidence after this statement).
- A possible cause of the problem is _________________. Perhaps a study which investigates ____________________ through ___________________ (method) could remedy this problem.
