# Cluster Sampling

Core Principle: Randomly select clusters; survey all members within the clusters.
Primary Applicable Scenarios: Surveys where units are widely dispersed and implementation costs are high
Type: Probability

The findings from the sample need to be generalized to the population, aiming for representativeness and objectivity. Allows for statistical inference.

# Procedure

1. The population is divided into natural clusters (e.g. classes, communities, factories). 
2. A subset of clusters is randomly selected, and **all** units within the chosen clusters are surveyed.

# Advantages

Significantly reduces costs and eases organization when the population is geographically dispersed, making simple random sampling impractical.

# Disadvantages

The sample is relatively concentrated. If units within clusters are very similar, sampling error can be larger.

# Scenario

Large-scale national surveys

# Example

Surveying elementary school students' eyesight nationwide by first randomly selecting several cities, then randomly selecting schools within those cities, and finally examining **all** students in the selected schools.
