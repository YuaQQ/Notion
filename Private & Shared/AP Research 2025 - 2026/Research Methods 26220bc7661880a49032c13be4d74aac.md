# Action Research

---

## Brief Description

Action research is a useful way of doing research if you are a practitioner who wishes to improve your understanding of your practice.

## Approach

- Explore
- Explain

## Design

- Experimental
- Non-experimental

## Method

Usually qualitative but can be quantitative

## Examples

(Common process: Identify problem → Test solution → Evaluate → Improve)

- Education: Teacher improves reading comprehension by testing graphic organizers, tracking student progress, and adjusting methods.
- Healthcare: Nurse reduces medication errors by observing workflow, introducing checklists, and measuring error rates.
- Business: Manager boosts team productivity by trialing flexible schedules, gathering feedback, and refining policies.
- Community Development: Nonprofit increases food bank usage by surveying needs, piloting delivery services, and scaling what works.

# Case Study Research

## Brief Description

Case study research is an in-depth investigation of a single individual, group, event, or organization to explore complex issues in real-world contexts. It is particularly useful when the boundaries between the phenomenon and its context are not clearly defined.

## Approach

- Explore: Used to understand a phenomenon when little is known.
- Explain: Helps uncover causal relationships or underlying principles in specific cases.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Case studies typically do not involve controlled experiments but instead rely on naturalistic observation and detailed analysis.

## Method

Usually qualitative, but can include quantitative data.

- Often involves interviews, observations, and document analysis (qualitative).
- May also incorporate surveys or statistical data (quantitative) in mixed-method approaches.

## Examples

- Business: Analyzing Tesla’s innovation strategy by examining its patents, leadership, and market disruption.
- Psychology: Studying Phineas Gage’s brain injury to understand frontal lobe function and personality changes.
- Education: Exploring Finland’s education system to identify policies behind its high student performance.
- Healthcare: Investigating a rare disease patient’s treatment journey to improve diagnostic protocols.

# Casual-Comparative Research

---

## Brief Description

Casual-comparative research (also called ex post facto research) examines potential cause-and-effect relationships by comparing groups where the independent variable has already occurred. It is used when experimental manipulation is not possible.

## Approach

- Explain

Focuses on identifying possible causal relationships after the fact, rather than exploring new phenomena or creating interventions.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Since the researcher does not manipulate the independent variable (it has already occurred), this design relies on comparing pre-existing groups.

## Method

Usually quantitative

- Involves statistical analysis to compare groups and infer possible causes (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA).
- Can sometimes include qualitative elements for deeper context, but primarily quantitative.

## Examples

- Sociology: Studying crime rates in neighborhoods with vs. without community policing programs.
- Environmental Science: Analyzing coral reef health near industrialized coasts vs. protected marine areas.

# Content Analysis

---

## Brief Description

Content analysis is a systematic research method used to analyze and interpret the meaning of textual, visual, or audio communication. It helps identify patterns, themes, biases, or trends within qualitative or quantitative data.

## Approach

- Explore: Uncovers recurring themes, language use, or cultural trends in data.
- Explain: Tests hypotheses about communication patterns, representations, or media effects.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Researchers observe and categorize existing content without manipulation (e.g., news articles, social media posts, speeches).

## Method

Qualitative or quantitative

- Qualitative: Focuses on interpreting meanings, contexts, and narratives (e.g., thematic analysis).
- Quantitative: Counts and statistically analyzes word frequencies, categories, or coded variables.

## Examples

- Media Studies: Analyzing gender stereotypes in 100 Disney films by coding character dialogues and roles.
- Political Science: Tracking framing of climate change in presidential speeches (2010–2024) to identify partisan language patterns.
- Historical Research: Studying propaganda techniques in WWII posters through visual symbolism and text analysis.

# Correlational Research

---

## Brief Description

Correlational research examines the relationship between two or more variables to determine if a statistical association exists, without implying causation. It helps identify patterns and predict outcomes.

## Approach

- Explore: Uncovers potential relationships between variables.
- Explain: Tests hypotheses about how variables may be related.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Variables are measured as they naturally occur, with no manipulation by the researcher.

## Method

Quantitative

- Uses statistical analysis (e.g., Pearson’s r, regression) to measure the strength and direction of relationships.
- Qualitative data is rarely used, but may supplement findings in mixed-methods designs.

## Examples

- Psychology: Linking social media use and self-esteem scores in teens (without claiming causation).
- Sports Science: Relationship between athletes’ sleep duration and sprint performance times.
- Neuroscience: Association between meditation frequency and gray matter density in brain scans.
- Music Therapy: Correlation between music tempo preferences and patients’ anxiety reduction levels.

# Critical Incident Technique

---

## Brief Description

The critical incident technique is a qualitative research method that collects and analyzes significant events ("critical incidents") to understand behaviors, decisions, or outcomes in a specific context. It is widely used in psychology, organizational studies, and education to identify effective or problematic practices.

## Approach

- Explore: Uncovers key behaviors or factors that influence success or failure in a given situation.
- Explain: Helps understand why certain incidents had significant impacts.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Relies on retrospective accounts or observations of real-life events without manipulation.

## Method

Primarily qualitative

- Involves in-depth interviews, observations, or written accounts to analyze incidents.
- Can incorporate quantitative elements (e.g., frequency counts of incidents) in mixed-method approaches.

## Examples

- Education: Examining "turning point" moments in teacher-student interactions that boosted learning outcomes.
- Healthcare: Identifying communication breakdowns in emergency rooms through staff interviews about critical patient cases.
- Cyber Security: Investigating security breaches via employee accounts of phishing attack responses.

[Critical Incident Technique](Research%20Methods/Critical%20Incident%20Technique%2026820bc76618808a88b0d8f0fb940e03.md)

# Delphi Research

---

## Brief Description

Delphi research is a structured, iterative method that gathers expert opinions to achieve consensus on complex issues or forecast future trends. It is particularly useful when objective data is limited or when geographical dispersion of experts makes face-to-face collaboration difficult.

## Approach

- Explore: Identifies diverse perspectives on uncertain or complex topics.
- Explain: Clarifies reasons behind expert judgments.
- Create: Develops consensus-based solutions or forecasts.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Relies on multiple rounds of questionnaires or surveys with controlled feedback, without variable manipulation.

## Method

Primarily qualitative (expert judgments), but can incorporate quantitative analysis (e.g., statistical consensus measures).

## Examples

- Technology Forecasting: Predicting AI adoption barriers in education through iterative expert surveys.
- Healthcare Policy: Consensus-building among 50 experts on future pandemic preparedness strategies.
- Disaster Management: Identifying key priorities for earthquake response plans with geologists and emergency responders.
- Education Reform: Prioritizing 21st-century skills for school curricula via multi-round teacher/administrator input.

[Delphi Research](Research%20Methods/Delphi%20Research%2026820bc7661880a7bd0cee4a14827345.md)

# Descriptive Research

---

## Brief Description

Descriptive research systematically observes and documents characteristics, behaviors, or phenomena as they naturally occur, without influencing variables. It provides a snapshot of "what is" rather than "why" or "how".

## Approach

- Explore: Primarily used to describe patterns, frequencies, or trends in data.

## Design

Non-experimental

- No manipulation of variables; focuses on observation and measurement.

## Method

Can be qualitative or quantitative

- Qualitative: Case studies, open-ended interviews, or ethnographic observations.
- Quantitative: Surveys, observational checklists, or statistical analyses (e.g., census data).

## Examples

- Political Science: Mapping voter turnout by demographic in the 2024 election.
- Linguistics: Documenting dialect variations in rural vs. urban speech samples.
- Sports Science: Recording average heart rates of marathon runners by age/gender.
- Astronomy: Charting seasonal visibility of Jupiter’s moons with amateur telescopes.

[Descriptive Research](Research%20Methods/Descriptive%20Research%2026820bc766188022981fda4ea3ed9b7b.md)

# Ethnographic Research

---

## Brief Description

Ethnographic research is an in-depth qualitative method that studies cultures, communities, or social groups through immersive observation and participation. It aims to understand behaviors, beliefs, and interactions within their natural context.

## Approach

- Explore: Uncovers cultural norms, social dynamics, and lived experiences.
- Explain: Interprets the meanings behind observed behaviors and practices.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Naturalistic setting with no manipulation of variables; focuses on real-world observations.

## Method

Primarily qualitative

- Uses participant observation, interviews, field notes, and artifact analysis.
- Rarely quantitative, but may incorporate basic counts or surveys in mixed-methods approaches.

## Examples

- Sociology: Documenting street vendor networks in Mexico City to map informal economic survival strategies.
- Consumer Behavior: Participating in sneaker collector meetups to understand subculture values driving resale markets.
- Education: Embedding in a high school classroom to study how teens use slang to negotiate social status.
- Cultural Anthropology: Living with a remote Indigenous tribe for 6 months to document rituals and social hierarchies.

[Ethnographic Research](Research%20Methods/Ethnographic%20Research%2026820bc76618808fa1bfdf2b1f8b2e63.md)

# Experimental Research

---

## Brief Description

Experimental research is a scientific approach that investigates cause-and-effect relationships by manipulating one or more independent variables and observing their impact on dependent variables under controlled conditions.

## Approach

- Explain: Primarily used to establish causal relationships and test hypotheses.

## Design

Experimental

- Involves controlled manipulation of variables, random assignment, and comparison groups (e.g., randomized controlled trials).

## Method

Primarily quantitative

- Relies on statistical analysis to measure outcomes (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA).
- Qualitative data may supplement findings but is not the primary focus.

## Examples

- Medicine: Testing a new drug’s effectiveness by randomly assigning patients to treatment/placebo groups.
- Engineering: Assessing battery life in smartphones with/without a new cooling system.
- Education: Evaluating a new math curriculum by randomly assigning it to half of classrooms.

[Experimental Research](Research%20Methods/Experimental%20Research%2026920bc7661880788447e04a2b10b997.md)

# Grounded Theory Research

---

## Brief Description

Grounded theory is a systematic qualitative methodology that generates theories through iterative data collection and analysis. It focuses on developing conceptual frameworks directly "grounded" in empirical evidence rather than testing pre-existing hypotheses.

## Approach

- Explore: Uncovers social processes and patterns in data.
- Create: Develops new theoretical models explaining observed phenomena.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Emergent design evolves during the study with no variable manipulation.

## Method

Qualitative

- Uses constant comparative analysis, coding (open/axial/selective), and theoretical sampling.
- While primarily qualitative, may incorporate quantitative elements in mixed-methods adaptations.

## Examples

- Technology: Creating a theory of AI trust adoption through analysis of user feedback forums.
- Education: Generating a framework for teacher resilience based on stories from high-turnover schools.
- Sociology: Building a model of "digital nomad" identity formation from traveler narratives.

[Grounded Theory Research](Research%20Methods/Grounded%20Theory%20Research%2026820bc76618808780eef5966ab493a9.md)

# Hermeneutic Research

---

## Brief Description

Hermeneutic research is an interpretive qualitative approach focused on understanding the meaning behind human experiences, texts, or cultural artifacts. Rooted in philosophy, it emphasizes the contextual and subjective nature of interpretation.

## Approach

- Explore: Uncovers deeper meanings and perspectives in texts or human phenomena.
- Explain: Interprets how meaning is constructed within specific contexts.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Analyzes existing texts, narratives, or communications without manipulation.

## Method

Qualitative

- Involves iterative interpretation of texts, dialogues, or symbolic content.
- While fundamentally qualitative, may integrate quantitative elements in mixed-method studies (e.g., quantifying thematic frequencies).

## Examples

- Literature: Interpreting hidden cultural critiques in 19th-century novels through textual analysis.
- Law: Analyzing judicial biases by examining how judges interpret "reasonable doubt" in rulings.
- Theology: Unpacking layers of meaning in religious scriptures across historical contexts.
- Art History: Decoding symbolic meanings in Renaissance paintings through artist diaries and patronage records.

[Hermeneutic Research](Research%20Methods/Hermeneutic%20Research%2026920bc7661880d0a823dc40c58b4867.md)

# Historical Research

---

## Brief Description

Historical research systematically examines past events, developments, or phenomena to understand their causes, effects, and significance. It relies on primary and secondary sources to reconstruct and interpret history accurately.

## Approach

- Explore: Investigates past events to uncover patterns or forgotten narratives.
- Explain: Analyzes causal relationships or contextual factors shaping historical outcomes.

## Design

Non-experimental

- No manipulation of variables; focuses on analyzing existing records and artifacts.

## Method

Primarily qualitative

- Uses document analysis, archival research, and critical interpretation of sources.
- May incorporate quantitative elements (e.g., statistical analysis of census data) in mixed-methods approaches.

## Examples

- Medical History: Investigating the 1918 flu pandemic’s impact on public health policies through newspaper archives.
- Economic History: Examining Great Depression bank records to identify causes of financial collapses.
- Social History: Reconstructing daily life in Victorian London using diaries, census data, and court records.
- Environmental History: Mapping deforestation patterns in the Amazon using colonial land surveys and satellite imagery.

# Meta-Analysis Research

---

## Brief Description

Meta-analysis is a statistical method that systematically combines and analyzes data from multiple independent studies on the same topic to identify patterns, derive overall conclusions, and assess the strength of evidence.

## Approach

- Explain: Focuses on synthesizing existing research findings to draw more powerful, evidence-based conclusions.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Analyzes previously collected data from various studies without new variable manipulation.

## Method

Quantitative

- Uses statistical techniques to aggregate effect sizes, test heterogeneity, and identify trends across studies.
- While primarily quantitative, may include qualitative elements in mixed-methods systematic reviews.

## Examples

- Economics: Evaluating 200 datasets to quantify the relationship between minimum wage hikes and employment rates.
- Medicine: Pooling data from 50 clinical trials to determine the overall effectiveness of antidepressants.
- Psychology: Synthesizing 120 studies on the impact of mindfulness therapy on anxiety levels.

[Meta-Analysis Research](Research%20Methods/Meta-Analysis%20Research%2026a20bc7661880f09b3ee287ed6e1c31.md)

# Narrative Research

---

## Brief Description

Narrative research is a qualitative approach that focuses on collecting and analyzing stories or personal accounts to understand human experiences, identities, and cultural meanings. It examines how individuals construct and communicate their lived experiences through storytelling.

## Approach

- Explore: Uncovers personal perspectives, life histories, and cultural narratives.
- Explain: Interprets how individuals make sense of their experiences through storytelling.

## Design

Non-experimental

- No manipulation of variables; relies on naturally occurring narratives (e.g., interviews, diaries, oral histories).

## Method

Qualitative

- Uses thematic analysis, structural analysis, or dialogic/performance analysis of narratives.
- While primarily qualitative, may incorporate basic quantitative elements (e.g., frequency of narrative themes) in mixed-methods designs.

## Examples

- Psychology: Exploring trauma recovery through survivors’ personal storytelling.
- Sociology: Studying immigrant family stories to map cultural adaptation challenges.
- Anthropology: Preserving indigenous oral histories to decode cultural identity shifts.

# Needs Assessment

---

## Brief Description

Needs assessment is a systematic process for identifying gaps between current conditions and desired outcomes, typically used to inform program development, policy decisions, or resource allocation.

## Approach

- Explore: Identifies unmet needs or service gaps in a target population
- Explain: Analyzes root causes of identified needs and prioritizes interventions

## Design

Non-Experimental

- Focuses on describing existing conditions without variable manipulation
- Often uses cross-sectional designs to capture current status

## Method

Qualitative or quantitative

- Quantitative: Surveys, statistical analysis of existing data, or standardized measurements
- Qualitative: Focus groups, interviews, or community forums for in-depth understanding
- Frequently employs mixed-methods approaches for comprehensive analysis

## Examples

- Social Work: Assessing food insecurity patterns in a city to optimize food bank locations and offerings.
- Public Policy: Mapping transportation accessibility issues for disabled residents to inform urban infrastructure upgrades.
- Technology: Surveying user pain points with a software interface to prioritize feature updates.

# Phenology

---

## Brief Description

Phenology is the study of cyclic and seasonal natural phenomena (e.g., plant flowering, animal migration) and their relationship to climate/environmental changes.

## Approach

- Explore: Documents timing patterns in biological events
- Explain: Analyzes drivers (temperature, rainfall) of phenological shifts

## Design

Non-experimental (observational)

- Long-term monitoring of natural systems without manipulation

## Method

Primarily quantitative

- Statistical analysis of temporal data (e.g., regression models of bloom dates vs. temperature)
- Qualitative methods may supplement (e.g., indigenous knowledge integration)

## Examples

- Conservation: Protecting endangered species (e.g., sea turtles) by correlating nesting dates with ocean currents.
- Public Health: Forecasting allergy seasons by analyzing pollen release timing in North America.
- Climate Change Research: Tracking earlier cherry blossom blooms in Japan to study warming trends.

# Quasi-Experimental Research

---

## Brief Description

Quasi-experimental research examines cause-effect relationships when full experimental control isn't possible, using pre-existing groups rather than random assignment. It bridges observational and experimental designs.

## Approach

- Explain: Primarily tests causal hypotheses in real-world settings where randomization is impractical

## Design

Neither fully experimental nor non-experimental

- Key features:

✓ Manipulation of independent variable

✗ No random assignment (uses intact groups)

✓ Often includes control groups/comparisons

## Method

Primarily quantitative

- Uses statistical techniques (ANCOVA, propensity score matching) to control confounding variables
- May integrate qualitative data (e.g., participant interviews) in mixed-methods designs

## Examples

- Public Health: Evaluating the impact of a smoking ban by comparing lung disease rates in cities with/without the policy (natural experiment).
- Environmental Science: Studying deforestation impacts by comparing biodiversity in adjacent protected and unprotected forests.
- Economics: Measuring employment effects of a job training program by comparing participants (self-selected) to non-participants.

[Quasi-Experimental Research](Research%20Methods/Quasi-Experimental%20Research%2026f20bc7661880e39aa7cd6df4cf9a03.md)

# Trend Analysis Research

---

## Brief Description

Trend analysis research examines patterns, changes, and developments in data over time to identify consistent directions, fluctuations, or emerging phenomena. It helps forecast future scenarios based on historical data.

## Approach

- Explore: Identifies patterns or shifts in data over time.
- Explain: Analyzes factors driving observed trends or predicts future developments.

## Design

Non-experimental

- Observes and analyzes existing data without manipulation.

## Method

Primarily quantitative

- Uses statistical tools (time-series analysis, regression models) to measure and project trends.
- Qualitative methods (e.g., content analysis of reports) may supplement interpretations.

## Examples

- Economics: Tracking GDP growth, unemployment rates, or market trends.
- Public Health: Monitoring disease outbreaks or vaccination rates over decades.
- Climate Science: Analyzing temperature changes or extreme weather frequency.
- Technology Adoption: Studying the rise of social media usage or renewable energy adoption.
