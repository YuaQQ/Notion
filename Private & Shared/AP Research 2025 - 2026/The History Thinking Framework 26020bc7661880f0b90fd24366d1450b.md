# The History Thinking Framework

[Original Article](https://www.historians.org/perspectives-article/what-does-it-mean-to-think-historically-january-2007/)

1. **History is a distinct discipline with its own "habits of mind."** It is not merely the memorization of facts, dates, and names. Instead, it is a sophisticated way of thinking that must be consciously learned and practiced, akin to an "unnatural act" that does not come intuitively.
2. **The core of historical thinking can be distilled into five key concepts (the 5 C's):**
    - **Change over Time:** Understanding that some things change while others remain continuous, and analyzing the pace, direction, and significance of that change.
    - **Context:** The imperative to situate events, actions, and sources within their specific historical setting to properly understand them, avoiding the error of judging the past by present-day standards.
    - **Causality:** Forming evidence-based arguments about the reasons for historical change, which involves weighing multiple, often competing, factors rather than identifying a single, simple cause.
    - **Contingency:** The understanding that every historical outcome depended on a prior set of circumstances, and that had any one factor been different, the outcome could have changed. This rejects teleological (straight-line, predetermined) views of history.
    - **Complexity:** Embracing the moral, epistemological, and causal messiness of the past, resisting simplistic narratives, myths, nostalgia, and the desire for orderly, clear-cut stories.
3. **The purpose of this framework is pedagogical.** The 5 C's make the implicit, "craft knowledge" of professional historians **explicit**, providing a foundation for teaching students from kindergarten to university how to think like a historian.
4. **This way of thinking is active and evidence-based.** It is developed through engaging with primary sources, formulating and supporting arguments, and challenging historical interpretations found in secondary sources. Exercises like debates, role-playing, and analyzing photographs are central to this process.
5. **It fosters critical citizenship.** Teaching the history disciplinary approach helps create future citizens who can think critically about the past, understand its complexity, and recognize their own role and responsibility in shaping the future. It moves students beyond seeing history as a simple prelude to the present or a list of facts to be memorized.

### **Problem Statement Template that can be used to describe and narrow the researcher’s topic of inquiry**

---

- There is a problem in or with the understanding of ___________.
- Despite the belief that _______________________ (something that is general accepted in current scholarship) ___________________ (something that refutes accepted thinking) (provide supporting evidence after this statement)
- This problem has negatively affected our ability to understand _____________because ________________ (provide supporting evidence after this statement).
- A possible cause of the problem is _________________. Perhaps a study which investigates ____________________ through ___________________ (method) could remedy this problem.
